    Section 7.4 Chi-Square test for Variance

    In this section we will learn how to perform a chi-square test for variance and standard deviation. We will review using the chi-square distribution to test hypotheses about variance and standard deviation. We will also learn how to write the conclusion for the chi-square test in context.
    🔗

    Objectives

    At the end of this section you will be able to:
    🔗
    1. Write hypotheses involving variance and standard deviation.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Perform chi-square test for variance.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Perform chi-square test for standard deviation.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Write the conclusion for chi-square test in context.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 7.4.1 Chi-Square Hypothesis Test

    A one-sample \(\chi ^2\)-test for a population variance is used to test a claim about the variability of a quantitative population when the population is normally distributed. When the hypothesize claim refer to standard deviation we will use \(\sigma\) in the hypotheses and when the hypothesized situation refers to the variance we will use \(\sigma ^2\) in the hypotheses. In this test, the sample variance \(s^2\) is compared to a hypothesized population variance \(\sigma ^2\) to determine whether the observed variability is significantly different from the claimed value.
    🔗
    The test statistic is computed as \(\chi^2=\dfrac{(n-1)s^2}{\sigma^2}\text{,}\) where \(n\) is the sample size. When the population is normal, this test statistic follows a chi-square distribution with \(n-1\) degrees of freedom. We will use StatKey to calculate the p-values. The resulting p-value is then used to decide whether to reject the null hypothesis and conclude that the population variance differs from, is greater than, or is less than the hypothesized value.
    🔗

    Definition 7.4.1.

    A one-sample \(\chi^2\)-test for a population variance or standard deviation is a statistical method used to test a claim about the variance or standard deviation of a normally distributed population.
    🔗
    The test statistic is
    \begin{equation*} \chi^2=\dfrac{(n-1)s^2}{\sigma ^2} \end{equation*}
    where \(s^2\) is the sample variance, \(\sigma ^2\) is the population variance, \(n\) is the sample size, and with degrees of freedom \(n-1.\)
    🔗
    🔗
    When you are given the variance, the value is already squared. When you are given the standard deviation you need to remember to square the value.
    🔗
    review critical and p-values on StatKey
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 7.4.2 Examples of Chi-Squared Test

    A machine is designed to fill bags of flour so that the variability in bag weights have a population variance of \(1.5\) ounces squared. A quality control manager wants to test whether the true variance in bag weights is different from \(1.5\) ounces squared. A random sample of \(20\) bags is taken, and the sample variance is found to be \(2.4\) ounces squared. Test the claim at the \(\alpha=0.05\) significance level.
    🔗

    Example 7.4.2. Flour Bags.

    State the null and alternative hypotheses, verify the conditions for a one-sample \(\chi ^2\)-test for variance, compute the test statistic, find the p-value with StatKey, and state your conclusion in context.
    🔗
    Solution 1. Hypotheses
    Let \(\sigma^2\) be the population variance in bag weights.
    🔗
    The hypotheses are
    \begin{equation*} H_0:\sigma^2=1.5 \qquad H_a:\sigma^2\ne 1.5. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Test Statistic
    Since this is a test about a single population variance, we use a one-sample \(\chi^2\)-test for variance. The test statistic is
    \begin{equation*} \chi^2=\frac{(n-1)s^2}{\sigma_0^2} =\frac{(20-1)(2.4)}{1.5} =\frac{45.6}{1.5} =30.4. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 3. p-value
    The degrees of freedom are \(df=n-1=20-1=19\text{.}\)
    🔗
    For a two-tailed test with \(df=19\text{,}\) change the middle area to 0.95 so the combined area of the tails is 0.05, thus the critical values are approximately \(\chi^2=8.907\) and \(\chi^2=32.852\text{.}\) Since test value \(30.4\) is between these two critical values, the test statistic does not fall in the rejection region wo we will not reject the null hypothesis.
    🔗
    For the p-value click on Two Tail and enter the test value 30.4 on the right side, combine the tail area for \(p-value = 2(0.047)=0.094 \text{.}\) since the p-value is greater than \(\alpha = 0.05\) do not reject the null hypothesis.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 4. Conclusion
    Therefore, we fail to reject \(H_0\text{.}\) At the \(0.05\) significance level, there is not enough evidence to conclude that the population variance in bag weights is different from \(1.5\) ounces squared.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    A biologist is studying the consistency of seed weights from a particular plant species grown in a laboratory. Under standard growing conditions, the population standard deviation of seed weights is believed to be \(0.8\) grams. The biologist wants to test whether the true population standard deviation has increased under a new nutrient treatment.
    🔗
    A random sample of \(25\) seeds is collected, and the sample standard deviation is found to be \(1.1\) grams. At the \(\alpha =0.05\) significance level, test whether the population standard deviation has increased from \(0.8\) grams. Assume the population of seed weights is approximately normally distributed.
    🔗

    Example 7.4.3. Seedlings.

    State the null and alternative hypotheses, verify the conditions for a one-sample \(\chi ^2\)-test for standard deviation, compute the test statistic, find the p-value with StatKey, and state your conclusion in context.
    🔗
    Solution 1. Hypotheses
    Let \(\sigma\) be the population standard deviation of seed weights.
    \begin{equation*} H_0:\sigma=0.8 \qquad H_a:\sigma\gt 0.8 \end{equation*}
    Since the chi-square procedure is based on variance, we can write the hypotheses in terms of \(\sigma^2\text{.}\) Equivalently, these may be written as
    \begin{equation*} H_0:\sigma^2=0.64 \qquad H_a:\sigma^2\gt 0.64. \end{equation*}
    This will be a right-tailed test.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Test Statistic
    The sample size is \(n=25\text{,}\) so the degrees of freedom are \(df=n-1=24\text{.}\) The sample standard deviation is \(s=1.1\text{,}\) so the sample variance is \(s^2=(1.1)^2=1.21 \)
    🔗
    The test statistic is
    \begin{equation*} \chi^2=\frac{(n-1)s^2}{\sigma_0^2} =\frac{(25-1)(1.21)}{0.64} =\frac{24(1.21)}{0.64} =\frac{29.04}{0.64} =45.375. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 3. p-value
    For a right-tailed test with \(df=24\) and \(\alpha=0.05\text{,}\) the right critical value \(\chi^2 =36.415\text{.}\) The test value of 45.375 lands in the reject region so we will reject the null hypothesis.
    🔗
    For the p-value click on Right Tail and enter the test value 45.375 along the bottom to get the area of \(p-value=0.0053\text{.}\) Since the p-value is less than \(\alpha=.05\) we will reject the null hypothesis.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 4. Conclusion
    Since we rejected \(H_0\) at the \(0.05\) significance level, there is sufficient evidence to conclude that the population standard deviation of seed weights under the new nutrient treatment has increased \(0.8\) grams.
    🔗

    Subsection 7.4.3 Practice Problems

    Checkpoint 7.4.4. Rainfall.

    A climate scientist is studying the year-to-year variability in annual rainfall for a particular region. Historically, the population standard deviation of annual rainfall has been \(3.2\) inches. The scientist wants to test whether climate change has altered the variability in annual rainfall.
    🔗
    A random sample of \(18\) recent years is selected, and the sample standard deviation of annual rainfall is found to be \(4.5\) inches. At the \(0.05\) significance level, test whether the population standard deviation differs from \(3.2\) inches. Assume the distribution of annual rainfall is approximately normal.
    🔗
    Solution 1. Hypotheses
    Let \(\sigma\) be the population standard deviation of annual rainfall.
    \begin{equation*} H_0:\sigma=3.2 \qquad H_a:\sigma\ne 3.2 \end{equation*}
    Since the chi-square procedure is based on variance, we can also write the hypotheses in terms of variance:
    \begin{equation*} H_0:\sigma^2=10.24 \qquad H_a:\sigma^2\ne 10.24. \end{equation*}
    This is a two-tailed test.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Statistical Test
    The sample size is \(n=18\text{,}\) so the degrees of freedom are \(df=n-1=17\text{.}\) The sample standard deviation is \(s=4.5\text{,}\) so the sample variance is \(s^2=(4.5)^2=20.25 \text{.}\)
    🔗
    The test statistic is
    \begin{equation*} \chi^2=\frac{(n-1)s^2}{\sigma_0^2} =\frac{(18-1)(20.25)}{10.24} =\frac{17(20.25)}{10.24} =\frac{344.25}{10.24} \approx 33.62. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 3. p-value
    For a two-tailed test with \(df=17\) and \(\alpha=0.05\text{,}\) the critical values are a \(\chi^2_L=7.564\) and \(\chi^2_R=30.191\text{.}\) Since the test statistic \(33.62\) is greater than the upper critical value \(30.19\text{,}\) it falls in the rejection region and we reject the null hypothesis.
    🔗
    For the p-value click on Two Tails and enter the test value 33.62 on the right, the \(p-value=2(0.0094)=0.0188\) which is smaller than \(\alpha = 0.05 \) so we reject the null hypothesis.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 4. Conclusion
    Since we reject \(H_0\) at the \(0.05\) significance level, there is sufficient evidence to conclude that the population standard deviation of annual rainfall differs from \(3.2\) inches, suggesting that the variability in rainfall may have changed.
    🔗

    Checkpoint 7.4.5. Vapes.

    A public health researcher is studying the consistency of nicotine content in a particular brand of disposable vapes. The manufacturer claims that the population variance in nicotine content is \(0.25\) milligrams squared. The researcher wants to test whether the true variance differs from this claimed value.
    🔗
    A random sample of \(16\) disposable vapes is selected, and the sample variance in nicotine content is found to be \(0.49\) milligrams squared. At the \(0.05\) significance level, test whether the population variance in nicotine content differs from \(0.25\) milligrams squared. Assume the nicotine content is approximately normally distributed.
    🔗
    Solution 1. Hypotheses
    Let \(\sigma^2\) be the population variance in nicotine content. The hypotheses are
    \begin{equation*} H_0:\sigma^2=0.25 \qquad H_a:\sigma^2\ne 0.25. \end{equation*}
    This is a two-tailed test.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Statistical Test
    The sample size is \(n=16\text{,}\) so the degrees of freedom are \(df=n-1=15\text{.}\) The sample variance is \(s^2=0.49\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The test statistic is
    \begin{equation*} \chi^2=\frac{(n-1)s^2}{\sigma_0^2} =\frac{(16-1)(0.49)}{0.25} =\frac{15(0.49)}{0.25} =\frac{7.35}{0.25} =29.4 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 3. p-value
    For a two-tailed test with \(df=15\) and \(\alpha=0.05\text{,}\) the critical values are \(\chi^2_L=6.262\) and \(\chi^2_R=27.489\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Since the test statistic \(29.4\) is greater than the upper critical value \(27.49\text{,}\) it falls in the rejection region and we reject the null hypothesis.
    🔗
    for the p-value click on Two Tails and enter the test value 29.4 along the bottom. The \(p-value=2(0.014)=.028\text{.}\) Since the p-value is less than \(\alpha =0.05\) we reject the null hypothesis.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 4. Conclusion
    Since we reject \(H_0\) at the \(0.05\) significance level, there is sufficient evidence to conclude that the population variance in nicotine content differs from \(0.25\) milligrams squared.
