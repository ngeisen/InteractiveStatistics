    Section 6.3 Intervals for Variance

    Have you ever tried fitting a nut to a bolt and discovered they do not fit? Many parts need to fit together in manufacturing. It is important to keep the variation of the diameter of the products as small as possible, or the striations on the nuts and bolts. Otherwise, they do not fit tougher properly and must be discarded. Similar with medications, the amount of variation among different pills in a bottle should have approximately the same amount of medication to ensure patients receive the appropriate dosage. In this section we will investigate confidence intervals for variance and standard deviations.
    🔗

    Objectives

    At the end of this section you will be able to:
    🔗
    1. Identify the point estimate \(s\) or \(s^2\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Determine the chi-square values from the chi square distribution.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Construct and interpret confidence intervals for variance.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Construct and interpret confidence intervals for standard deviation.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 6.3.1 Understanding the Basics

    To calculate confidence intervals for variance we need the chi-square distribution. The chi-square distribution is similar to the t-distribution in that its distribution is a family of curves based on the degrees of freedom. The symbol for chi square is \(\chi ^2 \text{.}\) The chi square distribution is obtained from values of \(\frac{(n-1) s^2}{ \sigma ^2} \) when random samples are selected from normally distributed populations whose variance is \(\sigma ^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The chi-squared distribution is right skewed and cannot be negative. The values begin at zero and approach infinity. The total area under the curve is 1 or 100%.
    🔗
    Let us review the symbolic notation we will be using.
    🔗
    \(s\) = sample standard deviation
    🔗
    \(s^2 \) = sample variance
    🔗
    \(\sigma \) = population standard deviation
    🔗
    \(\sigma ^2\) = population variance
    🔗
    \(n\) = sample size
    🔗
    The assumptions to use the chi-square distribution for confidence intervals the variance and standard deviation are: 1) the sample is a random sample, and 2) the population is normally distribution.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 6.3.2 Chi-Square Distribution with StatKey

    We will be using StatKey to find the chi-square values, but we need two values since it is not symmetric like t orz. The degrees of freedom is equal to the sample size subtracted by 1, thus \(df=n-1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    In StatKey, under the Theoretical Distributions choose the \(\chi^2\) symbol.
    🔗
    The next step is to enter the degrees of freedom. Let us find the chi square values for a sample size of 10 for 90% confidence. Thus, degrees of freedom is \(df=10-1=9\) then click OK.
    🔗
    Since we are working with confidence intervals, we want the middle 90%. Click on Two Tail and change the middle percentage to 0.90. The two chi-square values are along the bottom. The left chi = 3.325 and the right chi = 16.919.
    🔗
    Practice finding the chi-squared values for different sample sizes.
    🔗

    Checkpoint 6.3.4. 90% for n=20.

    🔗
    Solution.
    Click on [Edit Parameter] to change the degrees of freedom to \(df=20-1=19\text{.}\) Click on [Two Tail] and change the middle area to 0.90. The left chi = 10.117 and right chi = 30.143.
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 6.3.6. 95% for n=15.

    🔗
    Solution.

    Subsection 6.3.3 Confidence Intervals for Variance

    Now that we have the building blocks, now we can construct the confidence intervals for variance and standard deviation.
    🔗
    Confidence Interval for Variance Formula
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{(n-1)s ^2}{right \chi ^2} \lt \sigma ^2 \lt \frac{(n-1)s^2}{left \chi ^2} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    To find the confidence interval for the standard deviation, we find the square root of the variance values.
    🔗
    Confidence Interval for Standard Deviation Formula
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sqrt{ \frac{(n-1)s^2}{right \chi ^2} }\lt \sigma ^2 \lt \sqrt{ \frac{(n-1)s^2}{left \chi ^2} } \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Example 6.3.10. Nicotine Variance.

    Find the 90% confidence interval for the variance of the nicotine content of cigarettes if a sample of 20 cigarettes have a variance of 2.25 milligrams.
    🔗
    Solution.
    First find the chi-square values for 90%. The degree of freedom = 19. Left chi squared= 10.117 and right chi squared = 30.143.
    🔗
    We are given \(n=20 \) and \(s^2=2.25\)
    🔗
    Substitute values into the formula we get
    🔗
    \(\frac{(20-1)(2.25)}{30.143} \lt \sigma ^2 \lt \frac{(20-1)(2.25)}{10.117} \)
    🔗
    \(1.418 \lt \sigma ^2 \lt 4.226 \)
    🔗
    We are 90% confident that the true variance of the nicotine content of cigarettes is between 1.42 mg and 4.23 mg.
    🔗
    We are going to round to the same number of decimal places as the statistics.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 6.3.11. Nicotine Content.

    Find the 90% confidence interval for the standard deviation of the nicotine content of cigarettes if a sample of 20 cigarettes has a variance of 2.25 milligrams.
    🔗
    Solution.
    First find the chi-square values for 90%. The degree of freedom = 19. Left chi squared= 10.117 and right chi squared = 30.143.
    🔗
    We are given \(n=20 \) and \(s^2=2.25\)
    🔗
    Substitute values into the formula we get
    🔗
    \(\sqrt{ \frac{(20-1)(2.25)}{30.143} } \lt \sigma \lt \sqrt{\frac{(20-1)(2.25)}{10.117} }\)
    🔗
    \(\sqrt{1.418} \lt \sigma \lt \sqrt{4.226} \)
    🔗
    \(1.1908 \lt \sigma \lt 2.0557 \)
    🔗
    We are 90% confident that the true standard deviation of the nicotine content of cigarettes is between 1.19 mg and 2.06 mg.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 6.3.4 Practice Problems

    Use the chi square distribution. Pay attention if you are given the sample standard deviation or variance. The variance is already squared. You do not square the chi-squared values, use them as written.
    🔗

    Checkpoint 6.3.12. Variance of Temperatures.

    A meteorologist wants to estimate the variance of daily high temperatures in a Colorado mountain town during July. A random sample of 12 daily high temperatures, measured in degrees Fahrenheit, is recorded below.
    🔗
    Assume the daily high temperatures are approximately normally distributed.
    🔗
    Construct a \(90\%\) confidence interval for the population variance \(\sigma^2\) of daily high temperatures using the chi-square distribution.
    🔗
    Compute the sample variance, state the degrees of freedom, find the needed chi-square critical values, and interpret the interval in context.
    🔗
    Hint.
    If the population is normally distributed, then a confidence interval for the population variance \(\sigma^2\) is
    \begin{equation*} \left( \frac{(n-1)s^2}{\chi^2_{\alpha/2}}, \frac{(n-1)s^2}{\chi^2_{1-\alpha/2}} \right). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    For a \(90\%\) confidence interval, use \(\alpha=0.10\) and \(df=n-1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    From the data, \(s^2 \approx 7.54\) and \(df=11\text{.}\) Using chi-square critical values \(\chi^2_{0.95,11}\approx 19.675\) and \(\chi^2_{0.05,11}\approx 4.575\text{,}\) a \(90\%\) confidence interval for the population variance is approximately \((4.21, 18.13)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    We are given a sample of \(12\) daily high temperatures:
    🔗
    \(78,\;82,\;75,\;80,\;77,\;84,\;79,\;81,\;76,\;83,\;78,\;80\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Because the population is assumed to be approximately normally distributed, we can use the chi-square distribution to construct a confidence interval for the population variance.
    🔗
    First, compute the sample mean:
    \begin{equation*} \bar{x}=\frac{78+82+75+80+77+84+79+81+76+83+78+80}{12}\approx 79.42. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Next, compute the sample variance. Using the formula
    \begin{equation*} s^2=\frac{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2}{n-1}, \end{equation*}
    🔗
    we obtain \(s^2 \approx 7.54.\)
    🔗
    The degrees of freedom are \(df=n-1=11.\)
    🔗
    For a \(90\%\) confidence interval, \(\alpha=0.10\text{,}\) so we use the chi-square critical values
    \begin{equation*} \chi^2_{0.95,11}\approx 19.675 \qquad \text{and} \qquad \chi^2_{0.05,11}\approx 4.575. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The confidence interval for the population variance is
    \begin{equation*} \left( \frac{(n-1)s^2}{\chi^2_{0.95,11}}, \frac{(n-1)s^2}{\chi^2_{0.05,11}} \right) = \left( \frac{11(7.54)}{19.675}, \frac{11(7.54)}{4.575} \right). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Simplifying gives
    \begin{equation*} (4.21,\;18.13). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Therefore, a \(90\%\) confidence interval for the population variance of daily high temperatures is approximately \((4.21, 18.13)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Interpretation: We are \(90\%\) confident that the true variance of daily high temperatures in this Colorado mountain town during July lies between \(4.21\) and \(18.13\) square degrees Fahrenheit.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 6.3.14. Winter Temperatures.

    A meteorologist wants to estimate the standard deviation of daily low temperatures in a Colorado mountain town during the winter. A random sample of 12 daily low temperatures, measured in degrees Fahrenheit, is recorded below.
    🔗
    Assume the daily low temperatures are approximately normally distributed.
    🔗
    Construct a \(90\%\) confidence interval for the population standard deviation \(\sigma\) of daily winter low temperatures using the chi-square distribution.
    🔗
    Compute the sample variance, find the confidence interval for the population variance, then convert it to a confidence interval for the population standard deviation. Interpret the interval in context.
    🔗
    Hint.
    First construct a confidence interval for the population variance \(\sigma^2\) using the chi-square distribution:
    \begin{equation*} \left( \frac{(n-1)s^2}{\chi^2_{0.95}}, \frac{(n-1)s^2}{\chi^2_{0.05}} \right). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Then take square roots of the endpoints to get a confidence interval for \(\sigma\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    From the data, the sample variance is approximately \(s^2 \approx 14.99\text{,}\) so \(s \approx 3.87\text{.}\) With \(df=11\text{,}\) using \(\chi^2_{0.95,11}\approx 19.675\) and \(\chi^2_{0.05,11}\approx 4.575\text{,}\) the \(90\%\) confidence interval for the population variance is approximately \((8.38, 36.03)\text{.}\) Taking square roots gives a \(90\%\) confidence interval for the population standard deviation of approximately \((2.89, 6.00)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    We are given the sample of winter low temperatures
    🔗
    \(12,\;18,\;9,\;15,\;11,\;20,\;14,\;17,\;8,\;13,\;16,\;10\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Since the population is assumed to be approximately normally distributed, we use the chi-square distribution to construct a confidence interval for the population variance and then convert it to one for the population standard deviation.
    🔗
    First compute the sample mean:
    \begin{equation*} \bar{x}=\frac{12+18+9+15+11+20+14+17+8+13+16+10}{12}\approx 13.58. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Next compute the sample variance:
    \begin{equation*} s^2 \approx 14.99. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    So the sample standard deviation is
    \begin{equation*} s=\sqrt{14.99}\approx 3.87. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The sample size is \(n=12\text{,}\) so the degrees of freedom are \(df=n-1=11.\)
    🔗
    For a \(90\%\) confidence interval, \(\alpha=0.10\text{.}\) The needed chi-square critical values are
    \begin{equation*} \chi^2_{0.95,11}\approx 19.675 \qquad \text{and} \qquad \chi^2_{0.05,11}\approx 4.575. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The confidence interval for the population variance is
    \begin{equation*} \left( \frac{(n-1)s^2}{\chi^2_{0.95,11}}, \frac{(n-1)s^2}{\chi^2_{0.05,11}} \right) = \left( \frac{11(14.99)}{19.675}, \frac{11(14.99)}{4.575} \right). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    This simplifies to
    \begin{equation*} (8.38,\;36.03). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Now take square roots of the endpoints to get the confidence interval for the population standard deviation:
    \begin{equation*} (\sqrt{8.38},\;\sqrt{36.03})\approx (2.89,\;6.00). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Therefore, a \(90\%\) confidence interval for the population standard deviation of daily winter low temperatures is approximately \((2.89,\;6.00)\) degrees Fahrenheit.
    🔗
    Interpretation: We are \(90\%\) confident that the true standard deviation of daily winter low temperatures in this Colorado mountain town lies between about \(2.89\) and \(6.00\) degrees Fahrenheit.
