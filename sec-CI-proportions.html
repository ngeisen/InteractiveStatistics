    Section 6.2 Intervals for Proportions

    Previously we investigated the eye colors of students. About 2% of the world population has green eyes and about 9% of the US population has green eyes. But many times, we do know the population parameters, so we need to use a sample to estimate it.
    🔗

    Objectives

    At the end of this section you will be able to:
    🔗
    1. Identify the point estimate \(\hat{p}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Determine the z-value from normal distribution.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Construct and interpret confidence intervals for proportions.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 6.2.1 Understanding the Basics

    A quick review on notation. The population proportion is \(p\) often read as a percentage, such as 27% of the people in the United States have blue eyes, thus \(p=0.27\text{.}\) The sample proportion is \(\hat{p}\text{.}\) From the class survey, 6 students out of 42 had blue eyes, thus \(\hat{p} =6/42=0.143 \text{.}\) This sample statistic is less than the population parameter. We may also want to know what proportion of the sample does not have blue eyes. Here we want to find the complement of blue eyes. We often use \(\hat{q}\) to represent the probability it does not happen. Thus, \(\hat{q} = 1-0.143 = 0.857\) .
    🔗
    Formula Summary
    🔗
    sample proportion \(\hat{p}= \frac{X}{n}\)
    🔗
    where X = number in the sample meeting the condition and n = sample size.
    🔗
    complement \(\hat{q} = 1- \hat{p} \)
    🔗

    Checkpoint 6.2.1. Brown Eyes.

    From the class survey, 28 students had brown eyes out of 42. Find p^ and q^. About 45% of the US population has brown eyes.
    🔗
    Solution 1.
    Proportion of brown eyes is \(\hat{p} =28/42=0.667 \)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2.
    Proportion not having brown eyes is \(\hat{q} =1-.667=0.333 \) or \(\hat{q} =14/42=0.333 \)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 6.2.2. Green Eyes.

    From the class survey, 5 students had green eyes out of 42. Find p^ and q^.
    🔗
    Solution 1.
    Proportion of green eyes is \(\hat{p} =5/42=0.119 \)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2.
    Proportion not having green eyes is \(\hat{q} =1-.119=0.881 \) or \(\hat{q} =37/42=0.881 \)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Creating Confidence Intervals
    🔗
    When we do not know the population parameter, we need to use a sample to estimate it. Our sample statistic \(\hat{p}\) is the point estimate for the parameter, p. Next, we need the margin of error to see how far off we allow our estimate. Since most samples involving proportions are larger, we will be using the normal distribution assuming we meet the criteria that \(n \hat{p} \ge 5\) and \(n \hat{q} \ge 5 \) to ensure it is large enough. We also assume the sample is a random sample and the conditions for a binomial experiment are satisfied (reduced to two outcomes, fixed number of trials, each trial is independent of the others, see section 4.5 for more details).
    🔗
    Margin of Error is \(ME=z* \sqrt{ \frac{\hat{p} \hat{q}}{n}} \)
    🔗
    Confidence Interval for a Proportion
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \hat{p} -ME \lt p \lt \hat{p} - ME \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Common z* values are as follows:
    🔗
    For 90% confidence use z = 1.645
    🔗
    For 95% confidence use z = 1.96
    🔗
    For 98% confidence use z = 2.327
    🔗
    For 99% confidence use z = 2.576
    🔗

    Example 6.2.3. How to find z* with StatKey.

    Find z* for 90% confidence using the normal distribution.
    🔗
    Solution.
    These can be found using StatKey, going under Theoretical Distribution, select Normal, click on Two Tail and enter the percentage as a decimal in the middle portion of the curve. For 90% enter 0.90 in the middle white portion. The z* values are along the bottom. For 90% confidence, z* = 1.645. Refer to Section 5.4 for more examples.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 6.2.5. Blue Eyes Interval.

    From the class survey, 6 students had blue eyes out of the sample of 42 UNC students. Find the 90% confidence interval for the true proportion of UNC students that have blue eyes.
    🔗
    Solution.
    First, check assumptions. We will assume we have a random sample of students who are independent of each other, and the probability will remain the same for each. We need to check that the sample is large enough, so 6 have blue and 36 do not have blue eyes so we meet the condition of at least 5 for each.
    🔗
    Next, find the values.
    🔗
    Proportion of blue eyes is \(\hat{p} =6/42=0.143 \)
    🔗
    Proportion not having blue eyes is \(\hat{q} =1-.143=0.857 \)
    🔗
    For 90% confidence use z = 1.645
    🔗
    Margin of Error is \(ME=z* \sqrt{ \frac{\hat{p} \hat{q}}{n}} \)
    🔗
    \(ME=1.645 \sqrt{ \frac{(0.143)(0.857) }{42}} = 0.0889 \)
    🔗
    Lower bound is \(0.143 - 0.0889 = 0.0541 \) and upper bound is \(0.143 - 0.0889 =0.2319 \)
    🔗
    The interval is \(0.0541 \lt p \lt 0.2319 \)
    🔗
    We are 90% confident that the true proportion of UNC students that have blue eyes is between 5.4% and 23.2%.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 6.2.6. Brown Eyes Interval.

    From the class survey, 28 students had brown eyes out of 42. Find the 95% confidence interval for the true proportion of UNC students that have brown eyes.
    🔗
    Answer.
    The interval is \(0.5251 \lt p \lt 0.8089 \)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    First, check assumptions. We will assume we have a random sample of students who are independent of each other, and the probability will remain the same for each. We need to check that the sample is large enough, so 28 have brown eyes and 14 do not have brown eyes so we meet the condition of at least 5 for each.
    🔗
    Next, find the values.
    🔗
    Proportion of brown eyes is \(\hat{p} =28/42=0.667 \)
    🔗
    Proportion not having brown eyes is \(\hat{q} =1-.667=0.333 \)
    🔗
    For 95% confidence use z = 1.96
    🔗
    \(ME=1.96 \sqrt{ \frac{(0.667)(0.333) }{42}} = 0.5251 \)
    🔗
    Lower bound is 0.667 - 0.1419 = 0.5251 and upper bound is 0.667 - 0.1419 = 0.8089
    🔗
    The interval is \(0.5251 \lt p \lt 0.8089 \)
    🔗
    We are 95% confident that the true proportion of UNC students that have brown eyes is between 52.5% and 80.9%.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Sample Sizes
    🔗
    When you are ready to conduct your own study, how do you determine how large a sample is needed? Basically, we will take the margin of error formula and solve it for the sample size, n.
    🔗
    Sample size \(n= \hat{p} \hat{q} (\frac{z}{ME})^2 \)
    🔗
    where z represents the z-value for the level of confidence, and ME is the amount of error being allowing. We will always round up to the next whole person. If \(\hat{p}\) is unknown we will use 0.5 which will give us the maximum sample size needed.
    🔗

    Example 6.2.7. Thrillers.

    From a class survey of 43 UNC students, 3 preferred Thriller movies. This sample was too small to meet the conditions for the normal distribution. So how large of a sample is needed for 95% confidence if the researcher wishes to be accurate to within 2% of the true proportion?
    🔗
    Solution.
    We are given \(\hat{p}=3/42=0.070\) and \(\hat{q}=1-.07=0.93\text{,}\) and ME = 0.02. For 95% confidence use z = 1.96
    🔗
    Sample size \(n= \hat{p} \hat{q} (\frac{z}{ME} )^2 \)
    🔗
    \(n= (0.07)(0.93) ( \frac{1.96}{.02} )^2 = 625.22\)
    🔗
    Will need a sample of at least 626 people to be 95% confident within 2% accuracy.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 6.2.8. Laundry.

    A Resident Advisor (RA) wants to estimate the proportion of students who wash their laundry weekly. He wants to be 90% confident and be accurate to within 3% of the true proportion. How large a sample size would be necessary?
    🔗
    Solution.
    Since no proportion is given, we will use \(\hat{p}=0.5\) and \(\hat{q}=1.0-0.5=0.5,\) 90% confidence means z = 1.645, and ME=0.03.
    🔗
    Sample size \(n= (0.5)(0.5) ( \frac{1.645}{.03} )^2 = 751.67\)
    🔗
    Will need a sample of at least 752 students to be 90% confident within 3% accuracy.
    🔗
    🔗
