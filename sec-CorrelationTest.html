    Section 9.1 Hypothesis Testing for Correlation

    Another area of inferential statistics involves determining whether relationship exists between two or more variables. For example, a medical researcher may be interested in whether increased caffeine in energy drinks is related to heart damage. A zoologist may want to know whether the birth weight of a certain mammal is related to its life span. In this section, we will be analyzing data with correlation and regression analysis.
    🔗

    Objectives

    At the end of this section you will be able to:
    🔗
    1. Determine if the correlation is statistically significant using hypothesis testing.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Make predictions using the regression line equation.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Compute and interpret the coefficient of determination.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Compute the standard error of the estimate.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 9.1.1 Review of Correlation and Regression

    We will begin with a review of correlation and regression, as discussed in Chapter 2 when we graphed data and found the summary statistics.
    🔗
    Correlation is a statistical method used to determine whether a linear relationship exists between two quantitative variables. This can be seen visually with a scatterplot, and the strength and direction of the relationship is measured by the correlation coefficient. Regression is a method used to describe the nature of the relationship, whether positive, negative, linear, or non-linear. The regression equation allows us to make predictions using either variable. The regression line is also known as the line of best fit since it minimizes the distances each point is to the regression line.
    🔗
    Recall that a positive relationship exists when both variables increase while a negative relationship exists when one variable increase and the other variable decreases. The correlation coefficient measures its strength on a scale from -1 to +1. Values near 0 indicate no linear relationship. The sample correlation coefficient is designated as r and the population value as \(\rho\) (rho) .
    🔗
    There are several types of correlation coefficients. We will be using the Pearson product moment correlation coefficient, named after the statistician Karl Pearson, who pioneered research in this area. There are several ways to compute correlation. One formula to compute the sample correlation coefficient is
    \begin{equation*} r=\frac{\sum \left(\frac{x-\bar{x}}{s_x}\right)\left(\frac{y-\bar{y}}{s_y}\right)}{n-1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Another is
    \begin{equation*} r=\frac{n(\sum{xy})-(\sum{x})\times(\sum{y})}{\sqrt{n(\sum{x^2}-(\sum{x})^2)\times(n(\sum{y^2}-(\sum{y})^2)}} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    There are no units associated with \(r\text{,}\) thus the value will remain unchanged if the \(x\) and \(y\) values are switched.
    🔗
    The good news is we will use technology to compute the correlation coefficient.
    🔗
    The regression equation is an equation that describes the linear relationship between an explanatory variable and a response variable. The least-squares regression line is written as \(\hat{y}=a+bx\text{,}\) where \(\hat{y}\) is the predicted value of the response variable, \(a\) is the y-intercept, and \(b\) is the slope. The slope is computed using \(b=r\frac{s_y}{s_x}\text{,}\) and the intercept is computed using \(a=\bar{y}-b\bar{x}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The slope indicates the change in the predicted value of the response variable for a one-unit increase in the explanatory variable. The y-intercept is the predicted value of the response variable when the explanatory variable is zero.
    🔗
    The y-intercept can also be calculated using
    \begin{equation*} a=\frac{\sum{y} \sum{x^2}-\sum{x}\sum{xy}}{n\sum{x^2}-(\sum{x}^2)} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The slope can also be calculated using
    \begin{equation*} b=\frac{n \sum{xy}-\sum{x} \sum{y}}{n\sum{x^2}-(\sum{x})^2} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The sign of the correlation coefficient and the sign of the slope of the regression line will always be the same.
    🔗
    Again, we will use technology to find the values for the regression equation.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 9.1.2 Correlation Test Basics

    In correlation and regression hypothesis testing, we use sample data to decide whether there is enough evidence of a linear relationship between two quantitative variables in the population. For correlation, we test whether the population correlation coefficient \(\rho\) is equal to zero, which would indicate no linear association. For regression, we test whether the population slope \(\beta_1\) is equal to zero, which would indicate that the explanatory variable is not useful for predicting the response variable with a linear model. In both cases, the goal is to determine whether an observed relationship in the sample is strong enough to conclude that a real linear relationship exists in the population.
    🔗
    A correlation test is a hypothesis test used to determine whether there is a linear relationship between two quantitative variables in a population. It tests the population correlation coefficient \(\rho\) using the null hypothesis \(H_0:\rho=0\) saying there is no correlation between the two variables and the alternative hypothesis is \(H_a: \rho \ne 0\) stating there is a significant correlation. The alternative hypotheses can also be one-tailed test, stating the correlation is greater than or less than 0. If \(H_a \gt 0\) the claim is a significant positive correlation while \(H_a \lt 0\) claims a significant negative correlation.
    🔗
    The assumptions for a correlation test are that 1) the sample is a random sample. 2) The data pairs approximate a straight line and are measured at the interval or ratio level. 3) The variables have a joint normal distribution. This means that for any given value of x, the y values are approximately normally distributed, and for any given value of y, the x values are approximately normally distributed.
    🔗

    Definition 9.1.1.

    A correlation test is a hypothesis test used to determine whether there is a linear relationship between two quantitative variables in a population. The test statistic is
    \begin{equation*} t=\frac{r\sqrt{n-2}}{\sqrt{1-r^2}} \text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(r\) is the sample correlation coefficient and \(n\) is the number of paired observations. The test uses a \(t\)-distribution with \(n-2\) degrees of freedom.
    🔗
    🔗
    When the p-value \(\lt \alpha\) we reject the null hypothesis in support of the alternative indicating a significant correlation. When we do not reject the null hypothesis, we conclude there is no significant correlation between the variables.
    🔗
    There are several other measures that are associated with correlation and regression techniques. They include the coefficient of determination, residuals, the standard errors of the estimate, and prediction intervals.
    🔗
    The coefficient of determination, denoted \(r^2\text{,}\) is a measure of how well the regression line fits the data. The coefficient of determination, denoted by \(r^2\text{,}\) is the proportion of the variation in the response variable that is explained by the linear relationship with the explanatory variable. Its value is between \(0\) and \(1\) and often written as a percentage when in context. Larger values of \(r^2\) indicate that the regression line explains more of the variability in the response variable.The coefficient of determination is the ratio of the explained variation to the total variation.
    🔗
    It is computed as \(r^2=\frac{SS_{regression}}{SS_{total}}\text{,}\) where \(SS_{regression}\) is the sum of squares due to regression and \(SS_{total}\) is the total sum of squares.
    🔗
    The coefficient of non-determination is the unexplained variation, and computed from \(1-r^2 \text{.}\)
    🔗
    A residual in regression is the difference between an observed value \(y\) of the response variable and the predicted value \(\hat{y}\) by the regression equation. It measures how far a data point lies above or below the regression line. The formula for a residual is \(e=y-\hat{y}\text{,}\) where \(y\) is the observed value and \(\hat{y}\) is the predicted value. The mean of the residuals is zero. The sum of the squares of the residual computed by using the regression line is the smallest possible value. For this reason, a regression line is also called a least-squares line.
    🔗
    Sometimes we may be interested in plotting residual values or the prediction errors. These values can be plotted with the x-values in a residual plot. The x-values are plotted on the horizontal axis and the residuals on the vertical axis. Since the mean of the residuals is zero, a horizontal line with a y coordinate of zero is placed in the graph when completed with R Studio. If the residuals are evenly distributed about the line, the relationship between x and y is linear. If the residuals plot increases or is curved, then the regression line is not suitable for predictions.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 9.1.3 Correlation Test Example

    A biologist wants to investigate whether there is a linear relationship between the length of a fish and its weight. A random sample of 10 fish is collected from a lake, and each fish is measured for length in centimeters and weight in grams.
    🔗
    At the \(0.05\) significance level, test whether there is a linear correlation between fish length and fish weight in the population.
    🔗

    Example 9.1.3.

    First graph a scatterplot to describe the type of relationship, compute the correlation coefficient to describe its strength, and determine if it significant using hypothesis testing.
    🔗
    Solution 1. Scatterplot and Correlation
    Let \(x\) be fish length and \(y\) be fish weight. From the scatterplot in StatKey we see a positive linear relationship. As the fish length increases, so does the fish weight. The correlation coefficient is \(r=0.997\) which indicates a strong positive relationship.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Correlation Test
    We want to test whether the population correlation coefficient differs from zero, indicating a linear relationship exists.
    🔗
    The null hypothesis is that there is no correlation \(H_0:\rho=0\)
    🔗
    The alternative hypothesis states there is a correlation \(H_a:\rho \ne 0\) for a two tailed test.
    🔗
    From the sample data, the correlation coefficient is \(r \approx 0.997\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The test statistic for correlation is
    \begin{equation*} t=\dfrac{r\sqrt{n-2}}{\sqrt{1-r^2}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    With sample size \(n=10\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} t=\dfrac{0.997\sqrt{8}}{\sqrt{1-0.997^2}} \approx 36.43\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The degrees of freedom are \(df=n-2=8\text{.}\)
    🔗
    If we go to StatKey, under Theoretical Distributions, select t, enter the 8 degrees of freedom, select Two Tail and enter the test statistic along the bottom of the graph.
    🔗
    The \(p\)-value is extremely small, so \(p \approx 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 3. Conclusion
    Since \(p \lt 0.05\text{,}\) we reject \(H_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    There is sufficient evidence at the \(0.05\) significance level to conclude that there is a linear correlation between fish length and fish weight in the population.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 4. Regression
    The regression equation is \(\hat{y}=2.432x-5.2\) where \(x=\text{fish length}\) and \(y=\text{fish weight}\) .
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 9.1.4 Using Technology

    A kinesiology researcher wants to investigate whether there is a positive linear relationship between forearm length and height in adults. A random sample of 10 adults is selected, and each person’s forearm length and height are measured in centimeters.
    🔗
    At the \(0.05\) significance level, test whether there is a positive linear correlation between forearm length and height in the population. We are going to solve this using a graphing calculator.
    🔗

    Example 9.1.6.

    First graph a scatterplot to describe the type of relationship, compute the correlation coefficient to describe its strength, and determine if it significant using hypothesis testing.
    🔗
    Solution 1. Input Data
    Press the [STAT] button and under the EDIT menu select 1:Edit and press [ENTER] to select it. Input the forearm length in L1and the height in L2. Make sure to keep as pairs.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Scatterplot
    Press the 2nd button and then Y= button to select STATPLOT. Select 1: Plot1 and press [ENTER] to select it. Make sure it says On, you may need to use the arrows to highlight it and press [ENTER]. Under the Type of graph, select the first image for the scatterplot. Identify where the data is located, the Xlist:L1 and Ylist:L2. You can choose what the graph points look like.
    🔗
    Next, press the [Window] button to adjust the scale of the graph. The forearm lengths are between 20 and 35, the heights are between 150 and 190. Then press the [GRAPH] button.
    🔗
    We can see a strong positive linear relationship between the forearm length and heights. As the forearm length increases, so does the person’s height.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 3. Correlation
    First, let us confirm the Diagnostics are turned on so the calculator will compute the correlation coefficient. Press the [2nd] button then [0] to select CATALOG. Scroll down to DiagnosticOn and press [ENTER] twice so it says DONE.
    🔗
    Press the [STAT] button and arrow once to the right to the CALC menu, select 4:LinReg(ax+b) where a = slope and b = y-intercept, then press [ENTER]. On the TI-83 Plus you need to identify where the data is located, press [2nd] and [1] to select L1, press the [,] button, then press [2nd] and [2] to select L2. Press [ENTER] to perform the calculations.
    🔗
    The linear regression equation is \(y=2.8666x+90.332\) where x = forearm length and y = height. The correlation coefficient is \(r=0.982\text{.}\) The coefficient of determination is \(r^2=0.964 \)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 4. Correlation Test
    Let \(x\) be forearm length and \(y\) be height. We want to test for a positive linear correlation.
    🔗
    The null hypothesis is there is no correlation. \(H_0:\rho=0\)
    🔗
    The alterative hypothesis is there is a positive correlation for a right tailed test. \(H_a:\rho>0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    From the sample data, the correlation coefficient is \(r\approx 0.982\) and the sample size is \(n=10\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The test statistic is
    \begin{equation*} t=\dfrac{r\sqrt{n-2}}{\sqrt{1-r^2}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} t=\dfrac{0.982\sqrt{8}}{\sqrt{1-0.982^2}} \approx 14.56 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The degrees of freedom are \(df=n-2=8\text{.}\)
    🔗
    On the calculator press the [STAT] button and press the arrow twice to the right to the TESTS menu and scroll down to E:LinRegTest and press [ENTER] to select it.
    🔗
    Identify where the data is located, Xlist:L1 and Ylist:L2, change alternative hypothesis to \(\rho \gt 0 \text{.}\) Scroll to Calculate and press [ENTER] to perform the test.
    🔗
    The correlation test statistic is \(t=14.564\) and the p-value is very small since it is in scientific notation, \(p-value \approx 0\text{.}\) Notice on the test the regression equation is written as \(y=a+bx\text{,}\) thus the equation is \(y=90.332+2.8666x\) . If you continue to scroll down, you will also see the correlation coefficient \(r=0.981\) and the coefficient of determination is \(r^2=0.964 \text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 5. Conclusion
    Since \(p \lt 0.05\text{,}\) we reject \(H_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    There is sufficient evidence at the \(0.05\) significance level to conclude that there is a positive linear correlation between forearm length and height in the population.

    Subsection 9.1.5 Correlation Practice Problems

    For each problem, construct a scatterplot, find the correlation coefficient, determine if it is significant, find the regression equation and residuals, and interpret the coefficient of determination.
    🔗
    A medical researcher wants to investigate whether there is a linear relationship between the amount of caffeine in a person’s daily energy drink consumption and a heart damage marker level measured during a health screening. A random sample of \(10\) adults who regularly consume energy drinks is selected. For each person, the researcher records the average daily caffeine intake from energy drinks, in milligrams, and a heart damage marker score from a blood test.
    🔗
    At the \(0.05\) significance level, test whether there is a positive linear correlation between daily caffeine intake from energy drinks and heart damage marker score in the population.
    🔗

    Checkpoint 9.1.14. Correlation Test for Caffeine and Heart Damage.

    First graph a scatterplot to describe the type of relationship, compute the correlation coefficient to describe its strength, and determine if it significant using hypothesis testing.
    🔗
    Solution 1. Scatterplot and Correlation
    Let \(x\) be daily caffeine intake from energy drinks and \(y\) be the heart damage marker score. Upload the data into StatKey or input the data into your calculator. You should see a positive linear relationship. As the amount of caffeine consumption increases, the heart damage marker score also increases. The correlation coefficient is \(r=0.828\) and the regression equation is \(HD = 0.074(Caf)+8.942\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Correlation Test
    We want to test whether the population correlation coefficient is greater than zero, indicating a positive linear relationship exists.
    🔗
    The hypotheses are \(H_0:\rho=0\) versus \(H_a:\rho \gt 0\) for a right tailed test.
    🔗
    From the sample data, the sample correlation coefficient is \(r\approx 0.828\) and the sample size is \(n=10\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The test statistic is
    \begin{equation*} t=\dfrac{r\sqrt{n-2}}{\sqrt{1-r^2}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} t=\dfrac{0.828\sqrt{8}}{\sqrt{1-0.828^2}} \approx 4.18\text{.} \end{equation*}
