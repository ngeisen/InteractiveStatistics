    Section 8.4 F-test for Variance

    An \(F\)-test for two variances is used to compare the variability of two populations by testing whether their population variances are equal or whether one population has a larger variance than the other.
    🔗

    Objectives

    At the end of this section you will be able to:
    🔗
    1. Write hypotheses for the difference in variance.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Calculate the test statistic and p-value for the F-test.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Write the conclusion of a test in context.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 8.4.1 Understanding the Basics

    The two-sample variance test is used to compare the variances of two populations to determine whether the populations have different amounts of variability. In an introductory statistics course, this test is typically carried out with the \(F\)-test for two population variances. The hypotheses are written in terms of the population variances, \(\sigma_1^2\) and \(\sigma_2^2\text{,}\) often to test whether they are equal or not. Pay attention to whether you are given the sample variances or the sample standard deviations. The null hypothesis is that the population variances are equal thus \(H_0: \sigma_1^2 = \sigma_2^2\text{.}\) The alternative hypothesis is that the population variances are not equal, thus \(H_a: \sigma_1^2 \neq \sigma_2^2\text{,}\) or one is greater than the other \(H_a: \sigma_1^2 \gt \sigma_2^2\) or \(H_a: \sigma_1^2 \lt \sigma_2^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The test statistic is
    \begin{equation*} F=\frac{(s_1)^2}{(s_2)^2} \end{equation*}
    where \(s_1^2\) and \(s_2^2\) are the sample variances from the two independent samples. Usually, the larger sample variance is placed in the numerator so that the test statistic is at least \(1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    If the null hypothesis is true and the populations are normally distributed, this statistic follows an F-distribution with \(df_1=n_1-1\) and \(df_2=n_2-1\) degrees of freedom. This test assumes that the two samples are independent, that the observations within each sample are independent, and that both populations are approximately normally distributed. Because the \(F\)-test is sensitive to departures from normality, the normality assumption is especially important. The resulting p-value is used to decide whether there is enough evidence to conclude that the population variances differ.
    🔗
    Recall that when the p-value \(\lt \alpha \) we reject the null hypothesis, otherwise there is not enough evidence to reject the null hypothesis and we conclude that there is no significant difference in variances.
    🔗

    Definition 8.4.1.

    A two-variance \(F\)-test is a statistical test used to compare the variances of two populations and determine whether there is evidence of a difference between them.
    🔗
    The test statistic is
    \begin{equation*} F=\frac{(s_1)^2}{(s_2)^2} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    where \(s_1^2\) and \(s_2^2\) are the sample variances from the two independent samples that are approximately normally distributed.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 8.4.2 F-Distribution

    The \(F\)-distribution is used when comparing two population variances. In a test for two variances, we form the ratio of two sample variances, \(F=\dfrac{(s_1)^2}{(s_2)^2}\text{,}\) and use the \(F\)-distribution to determine whether the observed difference in variability is large enough to suggest a real difference in the population variances. The shape of the \(F\)-distribution depends on two degrees of freedom, one from the numerator sample and one from the denominator sample, and it is used in hypothesis tests and confidence intervals involving population variances.
    🔗
    First, we will be using StatKey to find the critical values and p-values for the F-distribution, then we will look at finding them on the calculator. Recall the larger variance will be in the numerator so the degrees of freedom will be \(dfN = n_1 -1\) and the degrees of freedom for the denominator will be \(dfD = n_2 -1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    F-distribution with StatKey
    🔗
    To find the critical values for a two-tailed test with an \(\alpha\) level of significance, we will need to find the upper tail critical value and the lower tail critical value. The upper tail critical value is found by entering the degrees of freedom for the numerator and denominator, then selecting the area in the upper tail equal to \(\alpha/2\text{.}\) The lower tail critical value is found by entering the same degrees of freedom, then selecting the area in the lower tail equal to \(\alpha/2\text{.}\) For confidence intervals, we will need to find the critical values for the desired confidence level by entering the the area \(1-\alpha\) in the middle section of the graph.
    🔗
    In StatKey, under Theoretical Distribution select F. Suppose the sample size for the numerator is 12 and the sample size for the denominator is 10 for a test for \(\alpha = .05\) testing for the difference in variance, which also suggests we want a 95% confidence interval.
    🔗
    The degree of freedom for the numerator is \(dfN=12-1=11\) and the degree of freedom for the denominator is \(dfD=10-1=9\text{.}\) Input the appropriate values and click OK
    🔗
    Since we are looking at a two-tailed test, check Two Tail and enter 0.95 in the center region for the critical values for the confidence interval or enter 0.025 in each tail for the hypothesis test. The lower critical value is 0.279 and the upper critical value is 3.912 along the bottom of the graph.
    🔗
    To find the p-value for a two-tailed test, we will need to find the area in the upper tail and the area in the lower tail. The area in the upper tail is found by entering the degrees of freedom for the numerator and denominator, then selecting the area in the upper tail equal to the test statistic. The area in the lower tail is found by entering the same degrees of freedom, then selecting the area in the lower tail equal to the test statistic. The two-tailed p-value is found by adding these two areas together.
    🔗

    Subsection 8.4.3 Examples to Compare Variance

    A school administrator wants to compare the consistency of travel times to school for students who ride the bus and students who are driven by a parent. To measure consistency, the administrator records the travel time, in minutes, for a random sample of students from each group over several mornings. The summary statistics are shown below.
    🔗
    Bus riders: \(n_1=12\text{,}\) \(s_1=4.8\) minutes
    🔗
    Parent drop-off: \(n_2=10\text{,}\) \(s_2=2.9\) minutes
    🔗
    At the \(0.05\) significance level, test whether the population variances in travel time are different for the two groups.
    🔗

    Example 8.4.14. Travel Times.

    State the hypotheses, compute the statistical test value and p-value, and write the conclusion in context.
    🔗
    Solution 1. Hypotheses
    Let population \(1\) be bus riders and population \(2\) be parent drop-off students. We want the larger variance in the numerator. We want to test whether the population variances are different.
    🔗
    The null hypothesis is the variances are equal
    \begin{equation*} H_0:\sigma_1^2=\sigma_2^2 \end{equation*}
    and the alternative hypothesis is the variances are not equal
    \begin{equation*} H_a:\sigma_1^2\ne\sigma_2^2\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Statistical Test
    The test statistic for a two-variance \(F\)-test is
    \begin{equation*} F=\dfrac{(s_1)^2}{(s_2)^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Using the sample standard deviations,
    \begin{equation*} F=\dfrac{4.8^2}{2.9^2} =\dfrac{23.04}{8.41} \approx 2.74\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The degrees of freedom are \(dfN=n_1-1=11\) for the numerator and \(dfD=n_2-1=9\) for the denominator.
    🔗
    Using an \(F\)-distribution with \(11\) and \(9\) degrees of freedom, the two-sided \(p\)-value is approximately \(2(.071)=0.142\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 3. Conclusion
    Since \(p-value=0.142 \gt 0.05\text{,}\) we fail to reject \(H_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    There is not sufficient evidence at the \(\alpha = 0.05\) significance level to conclude that the population variances in travel time are different for students who ride the bus and students who are driven by a parent to school.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    A manufacturing engineer wants to compare the consistency of fill amounts for two machines that package bottles for a popular sports drink. A random sample of bottles is taken from each machine, and the amount in each bottle is measured in ounces. The summary statistics are shown below.
    🔗
    Machine A: \(n_1=15\text{,}\) \(s_1=0.18\) ounces
    🔗
    Machine B: \(n_2=12\text{,}\) \(s_2=0.10\) ounces
    🔗
    At the \(0.05\) significance level, test whether the population variances in fill amounts are different for the two machines.
    🔗

    Example 8.4.16. Bottle.

    State the hypotheses, compute the statistical test value and p-value, and write the conclusion in context.
    🔗
    Solution 1. Hypothesis
    Let population \(1\) be bottles filled by Machine A and population \(2\) be bottles filled by Machine B. We want to test whether the population variances are different.
    🔗
    The null hypothesis is that the variances are equal
    \begin{equation*} H_0: \sigma_1 ^2= \sigma_2 ^2 \end{equation*}
    and the alternative hypothesis is that the variances are not equal
    \begin{equation*} H_a: \sigma_1 ^2\ne \sigma_2 ^2\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This is a two tailed test.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Statistical Test
    The test statistic is
    \begin{equation*} F=\dfrac{(s_1)^2}{(s_2)^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Substitute in the sample standard deviations:
    \begin{equation*} F=\dfrac{0.18^2}{0.10^2} =\dfrac{0.0324}{0.0100} =3.24\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The degrees of freedom are \(df_1=n_1-1=14\) for the numerator and \(df_2=n_2-1=11\) for the denominator.
    🔗
    Using the \(F\)-distribution with \(14\) and \(11\) degrees of freedom, the two-tailed \(p\)-value is approximately \(2(.028)=0.056\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 3. Conclusion
    Since \(p=0.056 \gt 0.05\text{,}\) we fail to reject \(H_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    There is not sufficient evidence at the \(0.05\) significance level to conclude that the population variances in bottle fill amounts are different for the two machines.
    🔗

    Subsection 8.4.4 Using Technology

    A hospital administrator wants to determine whether emergency room wait times are more variable on weekends than on weekdays. A random sample of wait times, measured in minutes, is collected for patients arriving on weekends and for patients arriving on weekdays. The summary statistics are shown below.
    🔗
    Weekends: \(n_1=14\text{,}\) \(s_1=18.2\) minutes
    🔗
    Weekdays: \(n_2=12\text{,}\) \(s_2=11.4\) minutes
    🔗
    At the \(0.05\) significance level, test whether the population variance in weekend wait times is greater than the population variance in weekday wait times.
    🔗

    Checkpoint 8.4.18. Wait Times.

    State the hypotheses, compute the statistical test value and p-value, and write the conclusion in context.
    🔗
    Solution 1. Hypotheses
    Let population \(1\) be weekend wait times and population \(2\) be weekday wait times.
    🔗
    The hypotheses are \(H_0: \sigma_1^2=\sigma_2 ^2\) versus \(H_a: \sigma_1^2 \gt \sigma_2 ^2\text{.}\) This is a right tailed test.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Statistical Test
    The test statistic for a two-variance \(F\)-test is
    \begin{equation*} F=\dfrac{(s_1)^2}{(s_2)^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Substituting the sample standard deviations gives
    \begin{equation*} F=\dfrac{18.2^2}{11.4^2} =\dfrac{331.24}{129.96} \approx 2.55\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The degrees of freedom are \(df_1=n_1-1=13\) for the numerator and \(df_2=n_2-1=11\) for the denominator.
    🔗
    Using the \(F\)-distribution with \(13\) and \(11\) degrees of freedom, the right tailed \(p\)-value is approximately \(0.064\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 3. Test with TI-83 Plus

    🔗
    Since we are given the summary statistics select Stats and press [ENTER] to select it, then input the summary values. Notice the calculator is requesting standard deviations.     A medical researcher wants to compare the variability in systolic blood pressure readings for patients using two different blood pressure medications.     🔗

    Checkpoint 8.4.31. Blood Pressure.

    State the hypotheses, compute the statistical test value and p-value, and write the conclusion in context.
    🔗
    Solution 1. Hypotheses
    This is an \(F\)-test for comparing two population variances.
    🔗
    Let \(\sigma_1^2\) be the variance in systolic blood pressure for Medication A and \(\sigma_2^2\) be the variance for Medication B. The hypotheses are \(H_0:\sigma_1^2=\sigma_2^2\) and \(H_a:\sigma_1^2\ne\sigma_2^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Statistical Test
    For Medication A, the sample standard deviation is \(s_1=1.87\) and the variance is \(s_1^2=3.5\text{.}\)
    🔗
    For Medication B, the sample standard deviation is \(s_2=6.77\) and the sample variance is \(s_2^2=45.83\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Using the larger sample variance in the numerator, the test statistic is \(F=\frac{45.83}{3.5}\approx 13.09.\)
    🔗
    The degrees of freedom for the numerator are \(dfN=6-1=5\)
    🔗
    and degrees of freedom for the denominator are \(dfD=6-1=5\) .
    🔗

    🔗

    🔗
    There is sufficient evidence at the 0.05 significance level to conclude that the population variances in systolic blood pressure differ between the two medications.
    🔗
