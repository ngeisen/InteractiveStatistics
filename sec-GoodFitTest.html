    Section 9.2 Chi-square Goodness of Fit Test

    A chi square distribution can be used for tests concerning frequency distributions. Such as, is the number of colors the same for each flavor in a bag of Skittles. It can also be used to see whether a frequency distribution fits a specific pattern. Suppose traffic engineer wishes to see whether accidents occur more often on some days of the week than others. When testing to see whether a frequency distribution fits a specific pattern, we can use the chi-square goodness-of-fit test.
    🔗

    Objectives

    At the end of this section you will be able to:
    🔗
    1. Test a distribution for goodness of fit using the chi square test.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Determine if frequencies are the same.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Determine if frequencies match a specific pattern.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 9.2.1 Understanding the Basics

    A chi-square test for one categorical variable is used to analyze the distribution of counts across categories and determine whether the observed frequencies differ significantly from what we would expect. In an introductory statistics course, this test is often used to decide whether sample data provide enough evidence to conclude that a population distribution does not follow a claimed set of proportions. By comparing observed counts to expected counts, the chi-square test helps us judge whether any differences are likely due to random chance or indicate a real difference in the population. A large chi-square statistic indicates that the observed counts differ substantially from the expected counts, suggesting that the claimed distribution may not accurately describe the population.
    🔗

    Definition 9.2.1.

    A chi-square goodness-of-fit test is used to determine whether the distribution of a single categorical variable in a population matches a claimed set of proportions. The test compares the observed counts in each category to the expected counts that would occur if the claimed distribution were true. The test statistic is
    \begin{equation*} \chi^2=\sum \frac{(O-E)^2}{E}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(O\) represents an observed count and \(E\) represents the corresponding expected count. The degrees of freedom for the test are \(df=k-1\text{,}\) where \(k\) is the number of categories.
    🔗
    🔗
    Suppose a candy enthusiast wants to test if the number of candies for each flavor in a bag of Skittles is the same. He opens a big of skittles and collects a random sample of 100 candies and organizes them into flavors with the following results: 22 strawberry, 24 orange, 14 grape, 17 lemon, and 23 green apple.
    🔗
    If there was no difference, one would expect each flavor to have the same frequency. In this case, \(100/5=20 \) candies per flavor is expected. Since the frequencies for each flavor were obtained from a sample, these actual frequencies are called the observed frequencies. The frequencies obtained by calculations are called the expected frequencies.
    🔗
    The observed frequencies often differ from the expected frequencies due to sampling error. But our question is, is there a significant difference between the frequencies or is it due to chance?
    🔗
    We are going to shift how we write hypotheses. The null hypothesis is a statement indication there is no difference or no change in the frequencies while the alternative states that the frequency is different than expected.
    🔗
    \(H_0:\) The flavor frequencies are equal.
    🔗
    \(H_a:\) The flavor frequencies are not equal.
    🔗
    In the goodness of fit test, the degrees of freedom are equal to the number of categories minus 1. In this case, there are five flavors for \(df=5-1=4 \text{.}\)
    🔗
    The formula for the chi square goodness of fit test is
    \begin{equation*} \chi ^2 = \sum{\frac{(O-E)^2}{E}} \end{equation*}
    where O = observed frequencies, E = expected frequency.
    🔗
    The assumption for the chi-square test is that the data are obtained from a random sample and the expected frequency for each category must be at least 5. Because the O-E values are squared, the answer will always be positive so every test will be a right-tailed test using the chi-square distribution.
    🔗
    It is helpful to create a table with the observed frequencies, the expected counts, the difference between the observed and expected values.
    🔗
    The test statistic is \(\chi^2 = \sum{\dfrac{(O-E)^2}{E}}\)
    \begin{equation*} \chi^2 = \frac{2^2}{20} + \frac{4^2}{20} + \frac{(-6)^2} {20} +\frac{(-3)^2} {20}+\frac{3^2} {20}=3.70 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Since the test statistic is relatively small for chi-squared, this means it is a good fit for having an equal distribution. If the test statistic is large, that indicates there is a larger difference between the observed and the expected values.
    🔗
    To find the p-value, we go to StatKey and under Theoretical Distributions select \(\chi ^2 \) and enter the degrees of freedom of 4, identify Right Tail, and enter the test statistic 3.70 along the bottom to get the p-value of 0.448, image below.
    🔗
    On the graphing calculator press [2nd] button and [VARS] to select DISTR and scroll down to 7: \(\chi^2\) cdf( then press [ENTER] to select it and then input 3.70 [,] E99 [,] 4 [)] then press [ENTER] to calculate the p-value = 0.448126. To get the EE press the [2nd] button then [,] button and then enter 99.
    🔗
    Since \(p \gt 0.05\) we do not reject the null hypothesis. We cannot conclude a significant difference in the number of candies for each flavor. Thus, there are approximately the same number of candies for each flavor. The chi-square test indicates it is still a good fit for having approximately equal frequencies.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 9.2.2 Examples for the chi-square goodness of fit test

    There are two main cases for the chi-square goodness of fit test that we will be investigating. 1) the frequency distribution is equal for each category or 2) it matches a specific pattern based on previous data. We will look at an example of both cases here.
    🔗
    Wildflowers
    🔗
    A hiking enthusiast is studying whether four species of wildflowers appear in equal proportions along some hiking trails in Rocky Mountain National Park. After randomly selecting and identifying \(120\) wildflowers, the hiker records the following observed counts:
    🔗
    At the \(\alpha = 0.05\) significance level, test whether the four species of wildflowers are equally distributed along the hiking trails.
    🔗

    Example 9.2.7. Wildflowers.

    State the null and alternative hypotheses, verify the assumption for a chi-square test, compute the test statistic, find the p-value, and state the conclusion in context.
    🔗
    Solution 1. Hypotheses
    Let the null hypothesis be that the four wildflower species are equally distributed. So we would expect 25% in each of the four categories.
    🔗
    The null hypotheses is
    🔗
    \(H_0:\) The flower species are equally distributed or \(p_1=p_2=p_3=p_4=0.25\)
    🔗
    And the alternative hypothesis is
    🔗
    \(H_a:\) The species are not equally distributed. Which means at least one of the proportions differs from 0.25.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Assumptions
    It was a random sample of flowers and there are at least 5 in each category, so we can use the chi-square distribution.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 3. Values
    Since there are 120 flowers and 4 categories, the expected count for each flower species under equal distribution is \(\dfrac{120}{4}=30\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 4. Good Fit Test
    The chi-square test statistic is \(\chi^2 = \sum{\dfrac{(O-E)^2}{E}}\)
    \begin{equation*} \chi^2 = \frac{(-6)^2}{30} + \frac{6^2}{30} + \frac{1^2} {30} +\frac{(-1)^2} {30} =2.467 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \chi^2=1.20+1.20+0.033+0.033=2.466 \end{equation*}
    from the table above.
    🔗
    The degrees of freedom from 4 categories is \(df=k-1=4-1=3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Using a chi-square distribution with 3 degrees of freedom, the \(p\)-value for a right tailed test is \(p=0.481\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 5. Conclusion
    Since \(p=0.481 \gt 0.05\text{,}\) we fail to reject \(H_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    There is not sufficient evidence at the \(\alpha = 0.05\) significance level to conclude that the four wildflower species are not equally distributed along the hiking trails in Rocky Mountain National Park.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Intersection Accidents
    🔗
    A traffic safety researcher wants to test whether the distribution of accidents among four types of intersections matches a previously reported pattern. Past transportation data suggest that \(20\%\) of intersection accidents occur at roundabouts, \(30\%\) at stop-sign intersections, \(35\%\) at traffic-light intersections, and \(15\%\) at other types of intersections. To investigate whether a more recent sample follows this distribution, the researcher records \(400\) intersection accidents and classifies each one by intersection type.
    🔗
    At the \(0.05\) significance level, test whether the population distribution of accidents differs from the reported distribution.
    🔗

    Example 9.2.18. Intersection Accidents.

    State the null and alternative hypotheses, verify the assumption for a chi-square test, compute the test statistic, find the p-value, and state the conclusion in context.
    🔗
    Solution 1. Hypotheses
    This is a chi-square goodness-of-fit test for a specific pattern. A random sample was taken and at least 5 accidents in each category.
    🔗
    The hypotheses are:
    🔗
    \(H_0:\) the population proportions are \(0.20\) for roundabouts, \(0.30\) for stop-sign intersections, \(0.35\) for traffic-light intersections, and \(0.15\) for other intersections, and
    🔗
    \(H_a:\) the population accident distribution is different.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Values
    Under the null hypothesis, the expected counts are the proportions multiplied by the sample size of \(400\text{:}\) Thus, we would expect \(400(0.20)=80\) in roundabouts, \(400(0.30)=120\) in stop-sign intersections, \(400(0.35)=140\) in traffic-light intersections, and \(400(0.15)=60\) in other intersections.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 3. Good Fit Test
    The test statistic is
    \begin{equation*} \chi^2=\sum \frac{(O-E)^2}{E}=1.80+1.633+0.257+1.067=4.757\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The degrees of freedom are \(df=k-1=4-1=3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Using a chi-square distribution with \(3\) degrees of freedom, the \(p\)-value is approximately \(0.190\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 4. TI-84 GOF-test
    Press [STAT] button and under the EDIT menu select 1:Edit and press [ENTER]. Input the Observed values in L1 and the Expected values in L2.
    🔗
    Press the STAT button and under the TESTS menu select D: GOF-Test and press [ENTER] to select it. Identify where the data is located and enter df=3 then press Calculate.
    🔗
    The statistical test value is \(\chi^2 = 4.757\) and p-value =0.190
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 5. Conclusion
    Since \(p=0.19 \gt 0.05\text{,}\) we fail to reject \(H_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    There is not sufficient evidence at the \(0.05\) significance level to conclude that the population distribution of accidents among roundabouts, stop-sign intersections, traffic-light intersections, and other types of intersections differs from the reported distribution.

    A political scientist wants to test whether the distribution of opinions among United States senators matches a previously reported national distribution of stronger gun laws. In 2022 the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act passed for expanded background checks after several school shootings. A recent Gallup report suggested that among Americans, \(60\%\) support strong gun laws, \(27\%\) are neutral or keep as now, and \(13\%\) oppose strong gun laws.     This is a chi-square goodness-of-fit test looking at a specific pattern.

    The hypotheses are \(H_0:\) the population proportions are \(0.50\text{,}\) \(0.30\text{,}\) and \(0.20\) for support, neutral, and oppose, respectively, and \(H_a:\) the population distribution is different.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Assumptions
    A random sample was taken and at least 5 are in each category, thus the chi-square distribution is appropriate.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 3. Values
    Under \(H_0\text{,}\) the expected counts are found by multiplying each proportion by the sample size \(100\) senators.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 4. Good Fit Test
    The test statistic is

    A city manager wants to test whether the distribution of calls received by the fire department matches a previously reported pattern. Past city data suggest that \(15\%\) of calls are for auto accidents, \(10\%\) are for fires, \(45\%\) are for medical emergencies, \(20\%\) are for public assistance, and \(10\%\) are for other calls. To investigate whether a more recent sample follows this distribution, the manager reviews \(300\) randomly selected fire department calls and classifies each one by type.
    🔗
    At the \(0.05\) significance level, test whether the population distribution of fire department call types differs from the reported distribution.
    🔗

    Checkpoint 9.2.29. Fire Department Calls.

    State the null and alternative hypotheses, verify the assumption for a chi-square test, compute the test statistic, find the p-value, and state the conclusion in context.
    🔗
    Solution 1. Hypotheses
    This is a chi-square goodness-of-fit test for a specific pattern. A random sample of calls was taken and there are at least 5 in each category.
    🔗
    The hypotheses are
    🔗
    \(H_0:\) the population proportions are \(0.15\) for auto accidents, \(0.10\) for fires, \(0.45\) for medical emergencies, \(0.20\) for public assistance, and \(0.10\) for other calls, and
    🔗
    \(H_a:\) the population distribution is different.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Values
    Under the null hypotheses, the expected counts are the proportions multiplied by the sample size of \(300\text{.}\) Thus, we would expect \(300(0.15)=45\) auto accidents, \(300(0.10)=30\) fires, \(300(0.45)=135\) medical emergencies, \(300(0.20)=60\) public assistance, and \(300(0.10)=30\) other calls.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 3. Good Fit Test
    The test statistic is
    \begin{equation*} \chi^2=\sum \frac{(O-E)^2}{E}=0.80+1.20+0.60+0.15+1.20=3.95\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The degrees of freedom are \(df=k-1=5-1=4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Using a chi-square distribution with \(4\) degrees of freedom, the \(p\)-value is approximately \(0.413\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 4. Conclusion
    Since \(p=0.413 \gt 0.05\text{,}\) we fail to reject \(H_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    There is not sufficient evidence at the \(0.05\) significance level to conclude that the population distribution of fire department call types differs from the reported distribution.
