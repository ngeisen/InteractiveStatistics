    Section 7.3 z-test for Proportions

    In this section, we will learn how to perform a one-sample \(z\)-test for a population proportion. This test is used to determine whether there is enough evidence in a sample of data to support a claim about the true proportion of a population that has a certain characteristic. We will go through the steps of hypothesis testing, including stating the hypotheses, checking assumptions, calculating the test statistic and p-value, and making a conclusion in context. We will also see how to use technology to perform these calculations and practice with real data examples.
    🔗

    Objectives

    At the end of this section you will be able to:
    🔗
    1. Write hypotheses involving proportions
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Determine the test statistic and p-value for the z-test for proportions.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Write the conclusion of a test in context
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 7.3.1 Hypothesis Testing for Proportions

    The one-sample \(z\)-test for a population proportion is used to test a claim about the true proportion \(p\) of a population that has a certain characteristic. In this test, a sample proportion \(\hat{p}\) is compared to a hypothesized population proportion \(p_0\) to determine whether the difference between them is large enough to be statistically significant. We will state the hypotheses and check that we have met the assumptions that 1) the sample is random or representative of the population and 2) the success-failure condition is satisfied, meaning that the expected numbers of successes and failures under the null hypothesis are both sufficiently large. We check that \(np \ge 10\) and \(n(1-p) \ge 10\)
    🔗
    Next, the test statistic is computed as
    \begin{equation*} z=\dfrac{\hat{p}-p_0}{\sqrt{p_0(1-p_0)/n}}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(\hat{p}\) is the sample proportion, \(p_0\) is the hypothesized value, and \(n\) is the sample size. The resulting p-value is the probability that statistical value or something more extreme is likely to occur if the null hypothesis is true and is used to decide whether to reject the null hypothesis to conclude that there is evidence for the alternative claim about the population proportion.
    🔗

    Definition 7.3.1.

    A one-sample \(z\)-test for a population proportion is a statistical method used to test a claim about a population proportion \(p\) using sample data. The test statistic is
    \begin{equation*} z=\dfrac{\hat{p}-p_0}{\sqrt{p_0(1-p_0)/n}}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(\hat{p}\) is the sample proportion, \(p_0\) is the hypothesized value, and \(n\) is the sample size.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 7.3.2 Hypothesis Test Examples for Parrots

    Researchers studying parrot vocal behavior recorded whether each parrot regularly interacted socially with other parrots in the dataset BenedictParrotVocals.csv. Suppose we want to test whether the proportion of parrots that regularly interacted socially with other parrots is greater than 50%.
    🔗
    In the sample, \(470\) out of \(892\) parrots were recorded as having “Yes” for the question “Social with other parrots?”.
    🔗
    At the \(\alpha = 0.05\) significance level, test whether the population proportion of parrots that regularly interacted socially with other parrots is greater than 50%.
    🔗

    Example 7.3.2.

    State the null and alternative hypotheses, verify the conditions for a one-sample \(z\)-test for a proportion, compute the test statistic, find the p-value, and state your conclusion in context.
    🔗
    Solution 1. Hypothesis
    Let \(p\) be the true proportion of parrots that regularly interacted socially with other parrots. The null hypothesis is the proportion is 50% while the alternative hypothesis is the proportion is greater than 50%.
    \begin{equation*} H_0:p=0.50 \qquad H_a:p \gt 0.50 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Assumptions
    The conditions for the one-sample \(z\)-test are satisfied because \(np_0=892(0.50)=446\) and \(n(1-p_0)=892(0.50)=446\text{,}\) both of which are at least \(10\text{.}\) So we can use the normal distribution since the sample is large enough.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 3. Statistical Test
    Use the sample proportion \(\hat{p}=\dfrac{x}{n}=\dfrac{470}{892}=0.5269\)
    🔗
    and the test statistic is
    \begin{equation*} z=\frac{\hat{p}-p_0}{\sqrt{\dfrac{p_0(1-p_0)}{n}}}. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} z=\frac{0.5269-0.50}{\sqrt{\dfrac{0.50(0.50)}{892}}} \approx \frac{0.0269}{0.0167} \approx 1.61. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 4. p-value
    For a right-tailed test, the p-value is \(P(Z \gt 1.61)\approx 0.054. \)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 5. Decision
    Since the p-value = 0.54 is slightly greater than the significance level \(\alpha=0.05\text{,}\) we fail to reject \(H_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 6. Conclusion
    There is not enough evidence at the \(0.05\) significance level to conclude that the proportion of parrots that regularly interacted socially with other parrots is greater than 50%.
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 7.3.3.

    State the null and alternative hypotheses, verify the conditions for a one-sample \(z\)-test for a proportion, compute the test statistic, find the p-value, and state your conclusion in context.
    🔗
    Hint.
    Let \(p\) be the true proportion of parrots with a mimicry repertoire size less than 15.
    \begin{equation*} Hypotheses: H_0:p=0.60 \qquad H_a:p \lt 0.60 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Use sample proportion \(\hat{p}=\dfrac{451}{892}\) and
    \begin{equation*} z=\frac{\hat{p}-p_0}{\sqrt{\dfrac{p_0(1-p_0)}{n}}}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    The hypotheses are \(H_0:p=0.60\) and \(H_a:p \lt 0.60\text{.}\) The sample proportion is \(\hat{p}=\dfrac{451}{892}\approx 0.506\text{.}\) The conditions are satisfied because \(np_0=892(0.60)=535.2\) and \(n(1-p_0)=892(0.40)=356.8\text{,}\) both at least \(10\text{.}\) The test statistic is \(z\approx -5.74\text{,}\) giving a left-tailed p-value of approximately \(0.0\text{.}\) Since the p-value is much smaller than \(0.05\text{,}\) we reject \(H_0\text{.}\) There is enough evidence to conclude that less than 60% of parrots have mimicry repertoire sizes of less than 15 words and phrases.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 1. Hypotheses
    Let \(p\) represent the true proportion of parrots whose total mimicry repertoire size is less than 15, where total repertoire size is the sum of mimicked sounds, phrases, and words.
    🔗
    The hypothesis are
    \begin{equation*} H_0:p=0.60 \qquad H_a:p \lt 0.60 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Assumptions
    From the sample, \(x=451\) parrots out of \(n=892\) had a total mimicry repertoire size less than 15. Therefore,
    \begin{equation*} \hat{p}=\frac{x}{n}=\frac{451}{892}\approx 0.5056. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    We verify the success-failure condition using \(p_0=0.60\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} np_0=892(0.60)=535.2 \qquad n(1-p_0)=892(0.40)=356.8. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Both values are greater than \(10\text{,}\) so the normal approximation is appropriate.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 3. Statistical Test
    Now compute the test statistic:
    \begin{equation*} z=\frac{\hat{p}-p_0}{\sqrt{\dfrac{p_0(1-p_0)}{n}}} =\frac{0.5056-0.60}{\sqrt{\dfrac{0.60(0.40)}{892}}} \approx \frac{-0.0944}{0.0165} \approx -5.74. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 4. p-value
    Because this is a left-tailed test, the p-value is
    \begin{equation*} P(Z \lt -5.74)\approx 4.33 \times 10^-9 \approx 0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 5. Decision
    Since the p-value = 0 is much smaller than \(\alpha=0.05\text{,}\) we reject the null hypothesis.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 6. Conclusion
    There is enough evidence at the \(\alpha =0.05\) significance level to conclude that less than \(60\%\) of parrots have mimicry repertoire sizes less than 15 words and phrases.

    Subsection 7.3.3 Using Technology

    Now let us use the TI-83 Plus Calculator to calculate the test statistic and p-value.
    🔗
    Researchers studying parrot vocal behavior recorded the sex of each parrot in the dataset BenedictParrotVocals.csv. Suppose we want to test whether the proportion of parrots identified as male is different from 50%
    🔗
    In the sample, \(430\) out of \(892\) parrots were identified as male.
    🔗
    At the \(\alpha = 0.05\) significance level, test whether the population proportion of parrots identified as male differs from 50%.
    🔗

    Example 7.3.4. Using TI-83 Plus.

    State the null and alternative hypotheses, verify the conditions for a one-sample \(z\)-test for a proportion, compute the test statistic, and p-value and state your conclusion in context.
    🔗
    Solution 1. Hypotheses
    Let \(p\) be the true proportion of parrots identified as male.
    🔗
    The hypotheses are
    \begin{equation*} H_0:p=0.50 \qquad H_a:p\ne 0.50 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Assumptions
    The conditions are satisfied because \(np_0=892(0.50)=446\) and \(n(1-p_0)=892(0.50)=446\text{,}\) both at least \(10\) so we can use the normal distribution.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 3. Statistical Test
    From the sample, \(x=430\) parrots out of \(n=892\) were identified as male. Thus, the sample proportion \(\hat{p}=\dfrac{430}{892} = 0.482\) and the test statistic is
    \begin{equation*} =\frac{0.4821-0.50}{\sqrt{\dfrac{0.50(0.50)}{892}}} \approx \frac{-0.0179}{0.0167} \approx -1.07. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    For the calculator click on the [STAT] button and use the arrows twice to the right to highlight TESTS across the top and scroll down to option 5: 1-PropZ Test and press [ENTER] to select the test.
    🔗
    Input the null hypothesized value \(p_0 = 0.50\text{,}\) the count of \(x=430 \) males from the sample \(n=892\text{.}\) Identify the alternative hypothesis of not equal, scroll to Calculate and press [ENTER]
    🔗
    The test statistic is \(z= -1.07\text{,}\) and the p-value is \(p=0.284\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 4. Conclusion
    Since the p-value=0.284 is greater than \(\alpha = 0.05\text{,}\) we fail to reject \(H_0\text{.}\) There is not enough evidence to conclude that the population proportion of parrots identified as male is different from 50%.

    Subsection 7.3.4 Practice Problems

    Checkpoint 7.3.9. Ocean Sounds.

    A wellness researcher wants to investigate whether more than half of adults feel that listening to ocean sounds helps them fall asleep faster. In a random sample of \(200\) adults, \(118\) said that listening to ocean sounds helps them fall asleep faster.
    🔗
    At the \(0.05\) significance level, test whether the population proportion of adults who feel that listening to ocean sounds helps them fall asleep faster is greater than \(0.50\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution 1. Hypotheses
    Let \(p\) represent the true proportion of adults who feel that listening to ocean sounds helps them fall asleep faster.
    🔗
    The hypotheses are
    \begin{equation*} H_0:p=0.50 \qquad H_a:p \gt0.50 \end{equation*}
    This is a right tailed test.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Assumptions
    We check the success-failure condition using \(p_0=0.50\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} np_0=200(0.50)=100 \qquad n(1-p_0)=200(0.50)=100. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Both values are at least \(10\text{,}\) so the normal approximation is appropriate.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 3. Statistical Test
    The sample size is \(n=200\text{,}\) and the number of successes is \(x=118\text{.}\) The sample proportion is
    \begin{equation*} \hat{p}=\frac{x}{n}=\frac{118}{200}=0.59. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The test statistic is
    \begin{equation*} z=\frac{\hat{p}-p_0}{\sqrt{\dfrac{p_0(1-p_0)}{n}}} =\frac{0.59-0.50}{\sqrt{\dfrac{0.50(0.50)}{200}}} =\frac{0.09}{\sqrt{0.00125}} =\frac{0.09}{0.0354} \approx 2.55. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 4. p-value
    The p-value for a right-tailed test is \(P(Z \gt 2.55)\approx 0.0054. \)
    🔗
    Since the p-value is less than \(\alpha= 0.05\text{,}\) we reject \(H_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 5. Conclusion
    At the \(\alpha = 0.05\) significance level, there is sufficient evidence to conclude that more than half of adults feel that listening to ocean sounds helps them fall asleep faster.

    Checkpoint 7.3.10. Uninsured Drivers.

    Transportation researchers want to know whether the proportion of drivers in a particular metropolitan area who drive without auto insurance is greater than the recent national rate of \(15.4\%\text{,}\) according the Insurance Information Institute(iii.org). In a random sample of \(400\) drivers, \(76\) were found to be driving without auto insurance.
    🔗
    At the \(\alpha = 0.05\) significance level, test whether the population proportion of drivers in this metropolitan area who drive without auto insurance is greater than \(0.154\) .
    🔗
    Solution 1. Hypotheses
    Let \(p\) represent the true proportion of drivers in this metropolitan area who drive without auto insurance.
    🔗
    The hypotheses are
    \begin{equation*} H_0:p=0.154 \qquad H_a:p \gt 0.154. \end{equation*}
    This is a right tailed test.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Assumptions
    We check the success-failure condition using \(p_0=0.154\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} np_0=400(0.154)=61.6 \qquad n(1-p_0)=400(0.846)=338.4. \end{equation*}
    Both values are at least \(10\text{,}\) so the normal approximation is appropriate.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 3. Statistical Test
    The sample size is \(n=400\text{,}\) and the number of drivers without auto insurance is \(x=76\text{.}\) The sample proportion is
    \begin{equation*} \hat{p}=\frac{x}{n}=\frac{76}{400}=0.19. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The test statistic is
    \begin{equation*} z=\frac{\hat{p}-p_0}{\sqrt{\dfrac{p_0(1-p_0)}{n}}} =\frac{0.19-0.154}{\sqrt{\dfrac{0.154(0.846)}{400}}} =\frac{0.036}{\sqrt{0.00032571}} \approx \frac{0.036}{0.0180} \approx 1.99. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 4. p-value
    The p-value for a right-tailed test is \(P(Z \gt 1.99)\approx 0.0233.\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 5. Decision
    Since the p-value is less than \(0.05\text{,}\) we reject \(H_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 6. Conclusion
    At the \(\alpha = 0.05\) significance level, there is sufficient evidence to conclude that the proportion of drivers in this metropolitan area who drive without auto insurance is greater than \(15.4\%\text{.}\)
